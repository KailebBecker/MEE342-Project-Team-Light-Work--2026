## Fabrication details  
The filament that we used was overture PLA. We used two printers, a BambuLab P1S  
and a BambuLab P2S. For the small hardware like the pin, brackets, and bottom mount  
was  
printed with 100% infill for the most stiffness. The head was printed with a low infill to  
reduce  
overall weight. The only two parts that needed to be reprint was the bottom mount  
because it  
snapped and the base because of printer errors. The mount was redesigned to be  
thicker and  
printed in a different orientation for more strength.  
## Assembly procedure and challenges  
One challenge we had was that we did not have a lot of options for springs that we  
could  
use, since we couldn’t order any springs online since the shipping process would take  
too long  
for the spring to arrive. Due to this, the spring that we did have access to had a higher  
spring  
constant value than we would have liked. Because of this, when first constructing the  
prototype,  
there was too much tension force from the spring on the upper arm, which caused the  
arm to be  
pulled back as far as it could go, and it caused bending in the arm as well. In order to  
reduce the  
amount of force from the spring, we had to change our upper arm, and reduce the  
distance of the  
pegs keeping the springs in tension. By reducing the distance between the pegs, it  
allowed for  
less spring displacement, which resulted in a lower force.  
Additionally, we realized that our lamp base struggled to keep the lamp upright while

significant

## Test procedures, results, and interpretation  
### Test Procedures:  
The team conducted several test procedures to identify the issues within the prototype.  
The lamp was rotated 360 degrees about the z-axis of the base to determine  
interferences with  
the base and the lamp mount. The lamp arms were tested sitting at various angles to  
test for  
bending, friction, and determine the effects of the force of the spring. The lamp head  
was rotated  
to determine the failure points near the rotating joint. The lamp head was positioned in  
the  
farthest position from the central axis of the base to maximize inertia and determine if  
the system  
would tip.  
### Results and Interpretation:  
The results of the test procedures provided a sway in the structure of the lamp. The  
spring  
force was too high for the system, causing the top beam to bend in the y-direction. The  
lamp also  
presented instability and tipping when the lamp arms were fully extended and in  
motion.  
### Interpretation:The prototype use of wooden beams resulted in lower strength and rigidity compared  
to  
the main design beams made of aluminum. Therefore, to achieve results similar to the  
original  
design, the project is based on, the beams would need to be increased in size. The  
spring force as  
well was calculated for the heavier weight of the original aluminum lamp design;  
because the  
prototype used wood and plastic material for the lamp it provided a lower weight. This  
caused an  
imbalance in the lamp’s system; therefore, the spring pegs were moved closer together  
on the top  
beam to create a smaller spring force. The tipping occurred in the prototype associated  
with the  
base not having enough weight; therefore, the team added rocks inside the base to  
increase the  
weight of the base and improve stability.

## Discussion  
While we did our best to predict and prevent part or performance failure in our lamp  
through design adjustments, there were still a few setbacks. The discrepancies  
between material  
characteristics for our actual design compared to our prototype gave way to a few  
surprises in  
terms of functionality of our prototype.  
Due to the weight and strength differences between Aluminum Alloy 6061 (the realistic  
design material) and the filament and MDF wood we used for our prototype, the  
balancing  
mechanics and component stresses had changed compared to what we calculated and  
simulated  
during our analyses. The weight of the components no longer balanced with the force  
of the  
springs, prompting us to make design changes to reduce the effect of the spring on the  
rest of the  
lamp. This spring strength also caused heavy elastic deformations in some of the swing  
arms  
when the lamp was in a specific position. While this wasn't necessarily a structural  
failure for the  
design, it is not a functional success. Lastly, the lightweight nature of the prototype  
affected the  
base of the lamp's ability to counteract the rest of the assembly to prevent tipping.  
Each of these  
issues were not originally accounted for because they would not have a significant  
impact on the  
lamp if it was made out of the intended material.  
The first failure that needed fixing was the balancing mechanism of the springs.  
Because  
the lamp was so lightweight compared to the true design, the springs were enacting too  
muchforce comparatively and actually unbalancing the lamp. By moving the spring's fixture  
location  
lower on the swing arm we reduced the magnitude of the moment it produces to a value  
closer to  
the moment caused by the weight of the structure. This allowed the springs to actually  
hold the  
lamp in place despite the lighter materials.  
To prevent the deformation of the upper swing arms we had to slightly adjust the  
materials for the prototype. Originally when the lamp was extended past 180o  
the springs would  
pull the assembly downward causing the wooden outer arm to bow outward in elastic  
deformation. The true design would be made out of a much stiffer aluminum alloy that  
wouldn't  
deform in this case. To prevent the deformation of the prototype we changed the  
wooden arm  
from three layered ¼” MDF wood slats to one ¼” wood slat layered with a ⅜” slat. By  
‘combining’ 2 of the bars in the arm it strengthened the arm and prevented bowing.  
This change  
in conjunction with the new spring attachment location has prevented any elastic  
deformation of  
the lamp in any position.  
To prevent tipping of the lamp we had to adjust the size of the base. The original design  
relied on the mass of the base to prevent tipping of the lamp, for the prototype  
however, the base  
was made hollow to save on materials and time, and since the filament is much lighter  
material  
to begin with we had to make some design changes to keep the lamp upright. This was  
as simple  
as widening the base and weighing it down with some rocks added into the base of the  
lamp. The  
added weight combined with the wider footprint enabled the base to counteract the  
weight of the  
rest of the lamp and prevent tipping.  
These unforeseen failures could have been prevented had further analysis been  
performed

resolved relatively quickly and easily it is also justified to solve problems like these assince they do not affect a finished product and apply only to the prototype.  
## Concrete list of design changes you would implement in a second iteration  
One design change the team would add are twistable pegs for the springs on each lamp  
arm. By being able to twist, users would be able to manually adjust spring tension,  
which would  
control how easily manipulable the lamp would be. This change would also be beneficial  
for  
counteracting fatigue in the springs over time, which is a critical point of failure in the  
system.  
Currently, the lamp head can spin into any position with just friction acting on it. A  
change the team would make in the future would be allowing the lamp head to spin on a  
type of  
ball and socket joint with a tightening screw, allowing the lamp head to be tightened  
into a  
locked position. This allows the user to have full customization of the lamp’s direction,  
without  
the need to rely on joint friction, which would wear over time.
